The adultification of black girls

This coincides with the adultification of Claudia. While mentally, she evolved, being forever trapped as a 14-year-old little girl was something the world kept on reminding her of.[...]

As Baby Lulu, Claudia plays a little child longing to be free. [...]

The play – written by the female members of the coven – can’t escape from being a tool to mock Claudia’s age, nor its minstrel-esque quality behind the performance with Claudia performing for someone’s entertainment again and again. She’s dressed like a child-like mammy figure, suffers from simulated violence every single night after being hit by a hammer, and even when she complains, having performed the play for the 500th time, Armand’s tight grip ensured Claudia obeyed. He demeans her, forcing her to live as the character off-stage by wearing the Lulu costume every night. The belittling and control is reminiscent of Child Q who, like Claudia, wasn’t protected but thrown to the wolves where she was racially discriminated against.

Institutional racism

Claudia is forced into a verifiable minstrel show at the Théâtre des Vampires, and when Louis and Claudia are “put on trial” it is clear their guilt is already decided, yet their white companion is offered a pardon. Claudia is “lynched” in front of the crowd, and the coven takes her dress as a souvenir and use her ashes to mix into makeup, as seasoning and even as a sexual aid, referencing a long history of the commodification of the black body.The monstrosity of the series is more overt than previous narratives, where monstrosity goes distinctly beyond the physical body, a black body, a monstrous body, and instead situates it firmly in the horrors of white supremacy.

Oppressive violence towards the black community

Lestat may have given Claudia a pivotal learning lesson: a warning that she shouldn’t get attached to mortals. Yet to inflict such trauma on a child is different altogether. It is treated as a humiliating punishment, no different from countless stories told and documented by history where Black people were forced to watch cruelty in front of their eyes (beatings and lynchings) to show what would happen if you stepped out of line. For Claudia, it was the realisation knowing she would never be treated as an equal, a context taken to another level by the growing examination of racial power dynamics in the first season. She watched Louis’ vulnerability exploited by Lestat’s extravagant dominance – and how the latter’s influence was weaving into her soul. It’s bad enough watching Louis wrestle his humanity, being repulsed by Lestat’s viewership of humanity as his playthings. Even worse when the immortal power is “gifted” by a White man.
