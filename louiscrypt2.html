Negative representation of protests in the media

This mirrors how right-wing Americans often viewed protests as inherently violence or riotous, regardless of if there was actual violence or rioting. As a 2021 study by the Department of Criminal Justice at SUNY indicates, consumers of right-wing news media sources were more likely to view protests as violent and support more law-and-order policing (Baranauskas 107). Of course, Louis’s violence here is fictional, but there is still the translatable element of negative public opinion in response to someone using drastic means to pursue their liberation.[...]

Lestat removes the political and/or social motivations behind Louis’s actions, instead insisting that this is his full acceptance of vampirism, and ultimately makes it about violence for the sake of self-gratification rather than as a way of liberating his community from a form of financial oppression. Again, this mirrors right-wing perceptions of 2020 protests—not as necessary demonstrations for the liberation of a community entrenched in a systemically racist society, but rather as violence or rioting for its own sake that accomplishes nothing.

Race and class intersectionality

The writers demonstrate the tightrope that Louis has to walk to maintain his status. He's forced to endure racialized insults because his peers don’t mean to hurl these pejoratives at him; they just mistake Louis for Black men who are not of his status. Louis remains cowed in the presence of the white men despite their backhanded compliments but ingratiates himself to them because they run the town, and he wants to expand his business. Lestat observing this blatant racism also makes him a kinder character than he is in the book. Watching Louis lower himself because of the laws of the land drives Lestat up the wall, and it’s one of the reasons why Lestat is desperate to turn Louis into a vampire — so that Louis can be everything he is "without apology." And that’s the crux of why race-bending the character works so well; telling stories about people of color, especially Black characters, who could have made strides were it not for the racist laws they had to live through.

Race and sexuality intersectionality

By including Louis’ queerness as a non-issue, this adaptation is aligning itself with fan interpretations of Louis and understanding how queer audiences have always seen Louis as queer without that being the crux of the story. Both of these interpretations are in line with Auerbach’s understanding of vampires as representative of their cultural moment of creation; as queer identities were more normalized, and racial issues came to the surface of our social climate, this was reflected in how sexuality was portrayed in this adaptation Interview, and this built upon a pre-established audience that has always seen Louis as openly queer. Regarding intersectionality, Louis’s intersectional shame and discrimination show that both racism and homophobia are embedded into American society, but the focus on Louis’s race more than his sexuality and Lestat’s attempt to free him from this by giving him the power of vampirism show that, in the cultural moment of the show’s creation, our society was much more focused on bringing to light and hopefully changing our systemic racism, and while homophobia was still present, it was relegated to the background.
